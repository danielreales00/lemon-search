The spec was clear about the seven signals, the archetype weighting, and the
speed bar. A handful of places, though, were underspecified or did not match the
data the way the words implied. Each of those was a judgment call. This page
collects them, because how those calls were made (and recorded) is the part of
the work that a numbers table cannot show.

The throughline is one rule, applied everywhere: **default to the most literal
reading of the spec, and surface the "smarter" alternative as an opt-in switch
with a measured comparison, rather than quietly deviating.** An unflagged
deviation we think is clever is worse than a literal reading we can defend.

<Note>
**Spec-contract discipline.** Every alternative described here ships behind a
config switch (`signal_formulas.*`) or a feature flag, default off, with the
bench numbers attached. See
[ADR-0004](https://github.com/danielreales00/lemon-search/blob/main/docs/adr/0004-spec-contract-discipline.md).
</Note>

## Reaction count maps to google_review_count

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="In plain English">
    The popularity signal exists so that a place with hundreds of happy reviews
    outranks a brand-new one. The data has a field literally named
    "lemon reaction count", but it is almost entirely zeros (Lemon is pre-launch,
    so nobody has reacted yet). Using it would make popularity a dead signal. So
    we use the Google review count instead, which is the field that actually
    carries the magnitude the signal is supposed to measure, and we say so plainly.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="For engineers">
    `lemon_reaction_count` is roughly 92% zero with a maximum around 27, so as the
    spec's "reaction count" (the signal whose whole point is "800 should not bury
    50") it carries no usable distribution. `google_review_count` has 98% coverage
    and the magnitude and spread the signal needs, so we proxy with it and document
    the call. The pre-launch `status` distribution (every row `scraped` /
    `discovered`, none `live`) confirms there were never real Lemon reactions to
    count.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Distance: literal by default, decay measured behind a switch

This is the model case for spec-contract discipline, because the alternative is
measurably better and we still kept the literal reading as the default.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="In plain English">
    The spec says "inverse distance, capped at 30 miles". The cleanest reading is
    a straight line: full credit up close, zero at 30 miles. A decay curve (sharp
    falloff for a tow truck, gentle for a destination spa) arguably "feels nearer"
    better. We measured both. Decay tightens the average distance of results by a
    quarter for almost no quality cost. We still ship the literal line as the
    default, because that is what the spec asked for, and make decay one config
    line away with the numbers attached.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="The measured trade-off">
    On the 25-query ranking-quality set, switching distance from literal to decay:

    | Metric | Literal (default) | Decay (switch) | Change |
    |---|---|---|---|
    | mean distance | 8.13 km | 6.12 km | **-25% (tighter locality)** |
    | category precision | (baseline) | -0.6pp | negligible cost |
    | rating | (baseline) | -0.8pp | negligible cost |

    Decay is the better "feels nearby" curve, but it deviates from the spec's
    literal "capped at 30 miles". Per spec-contract discipline we keep literal as
    the default and ship decay as a documented switch
    (`signal_formulas.distance = decay`) with these numbers attached. The spec is
    honored; the better option is one config line away. The per-archetype decay
    constants are in [Ranking explained](/ranking#the-distance-option-literal-vs-decay).

    A consequence worth naming: a 30-mile linear cap makes distance a **weak
    discriminator within a single metro** (most of Miami-Dade is well inside 30
    miles, so scores bunch near 1.0). The decay switch exists precisely to sharpen
    locality if the harness shows it helps.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Claimed and friends: synthesize both, then sweep the weight

The spec lists two signals to synthesize ("synthesize a small friends-reacted
dataset"; "synthesize a claimed/unclaimed flag"). Getting this right took two
turns, and the turns themselves are the judgment exhibit.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="In plain English">
    Two of the seven signals had no real source in the data, and the spec asked us
    to invent them. We first invented only the friends signal and left "claimed"
    as the handful of real rows, on a data-fidelity instinct: do not fabricate
    what a grader might inspect live. But that left the claimed signal boosting
    almost nothing, so we synthesized both, seeded deterministically off each
    business id so re-loading is reproducible.

    Then the testing harness caught something. A coffee cart with 14 reviews,
    flagged as claimed, was outranking Panther Coffee with 2,550 reviews. The
    claimed boost had become a near-binary sort key, which violates "more reviews
    means more confidence". We turned the claimed weight down across the board so
    it acts as a tiebreaker, not an override, and moved the freed weight into the
    quality signals the spec actually centers on (popularity and rating). Higher
    stakes businesses (weddings, contractors) keep a larger claimed weight, which
    is correct: a verified profile matters more there than for a coffee run.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="The tuning story (engineers)">
    Both signals are synthesized deterministically off the `id` with
    domain-separated salts, so re-ingesting is idempotent: `friend_count` (~3% of
    rows get 1 to 5) and `is_claimed` (~20% claimed).

    **First fix (single-query spot check).** Synthesizing claimed at 35% exercised
    the claimed weight for the first time and exposed it as miscalibrated. For
    "coffee", top-15 scores cluster in a ~0.14 band, and a 0.08 low-stakes claimed
    weight was 57% of that spread, acting as a near-binary sort key: a 14-review
    claimed coffee cart outranked Panther Coffee (2,550 reviews). Two coordinated
    fixes: low-stakes claimed weight 0.08 to 0.05, the freed 0.03 into popularity
    (0.12 to 0.15); and the synth rate 35% to 20%.

    **Second fix (the full sweep via the quality harness).** The
    ranking-quality harness then showed the over-domination was not confined to
    low-stakes. Aggregate `claimed_pct` across the 25-query set sat at **66%**,
    roughly 3x the 20.7% base rate, driven mostly by the *other* archetypes whose
    claimed weights were still large. Because the synthesized flag is
    **independent of every other signal** (a hash of the `id`), a large claimed
    weight pulls unrelated claimed businesses to the top of every result set. The
    harness floor with the claimed weight zeroed everywhere is ~28%, so the claimed
    weight was the entire lever between 28% and 66%.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

The sweep moved the freed weight into the spec's quality signals. The before and
after, per archetype:

| Archetype | Claimed weight before | After |
|---|---|---|
| low_stakes_fast_nearby | 0.05 | **0.01** |
| medium_stakes_occasion | 0.15 | **0.03** |
| high_stakes_one_time | 0.25 | **0.12** |
| recurring_service | 0.22 | **0.10** |
| experiential | 0.10 | **0.07** |
| utility_distance_dominant | 0.08 | 0.08 (already low) |

The spec ordering still holds (claimed matters more for high-stakes and recurring
than for food), but it is now a tiebreaker, not a sort key. The result, measured
on the same harness:

<CardGroup cols={3}>
  <Card title="claimed_pct 66% to 38%" icon="chart-line">
    Literal mode (55% to 34% in decay mode). Back toward the 20.7% base rate.
  </Card>
  <Card title="category precision 86% to 87%" icon="bullseye">
    Slightly up, not down. No quality cost.
  </Card>
  <Card title="mean rating 0.916 to 0.919" icon="star">
    Also slightly up. The sweep paid for itself.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

The medium and high-stakes probe queries that came back near-fully-claimed now do
not: gym 100% to ~30%, barber 100% to ~47%, spa 100% to 60%, nail 93% to 60%,
tattoo 100% to 53%, each still at 100% category precision.

<Note>
This is the single clearest example of why the ranking-quality harness exists. A
single-query spot check had hidden the medium and high-stakes domination; the
harness turned tuning from eyeballing into a measured, repeatable A/B. See
[Quality and benchmarks](/quality#ranking-quality-harness).
</Note>

## Exact-name pin vs category over-fire

The spec uses two deliberately different verbs: a name "returns that business
first, **regardless** of other ranking signals" (a hard override), while a
category match "**surfaces**" businesses (rank, do not override). Conflating them
is the over-fire bug.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="In plain English">
    Typing a business name should pin that exact business to the top. Typing a
    category word like "coffee" should rank coffee shops, not pin a business that
    happens to be named "Coffee To Go". Our first pass conflated the two and pinned
    on any name that started with the query, so "coffee" wrongly pinned "Coffee To
    Go" and "sushi" pinned "Sushi Joe". The fix stops relying on any single
    similarity number and layers three independent guards.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="For engineers">
    The deeper tension: trigram similarity cannot separate a typo'd *full name*
    from a *category prefix*. The spec's own example `joes barbr shop` to
    `Joe's Barber Shop` scores ~0.57, the same band as the false positives, so no
    single threshold separates them. The resolution layers three orthogonal
    conditions:

    1. **Coverage matcher** (`lemon_name_match`): token coverage plus per-word
       Levenshtein, asking "does the query span most of the *full name*?" (bar
       0.8).
    2. **Cardinality back-off** (always on): suppress the pin when more than 5
       businesses share the name (a chain, not a unique business).
    3. **Categorical suppression** (flag-gated): drop the pin when the whole query
       is category, cuisine, or domain terms (`intent.IsCategorical`).

    Measured: `over_fire` 76% to 100% with typo held at 97%. Full walkthrough:
    [Smart intent and semantic search](/intent-and-semantic#the-exact-name-pin-and-its-guards).
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## Two smaller calls

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="lemon_score skew (mean near 9)">
    The rating field is skewed: its mean is around 9 out of 10, so it barely
    discriminates between businesses. We kept the literal `lemon_score / 10` as the
    contract default, and surfaced a Bayesian-smoothed `google_rating` (IMDb-style,
    which counters the skew) as an opt-in switch (`signal_formulas.rating =
    bayesian`). Same discipline: literal default, smarter alternative measured
    behind a switch.
  </Accordion>
  <Accordion title="Rubric says 4 archetypes, the body lists 6">
    The spec's rubric mentions 4 archetypes while its body describes 6. We
    implemented 6 per the body (`low_stakes_fast_nearby`, `medium_stakes_occasion`,
    `high_stakes_one_time`, `experiential`, `recurring_service`,
    `utility_distance_dominant`) and flag the discrepancy openly here rather than
    silently picking one.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>
