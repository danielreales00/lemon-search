Ranking is where Lemon Search decides the order of results. Every candidate is
scored on seven signals, each normalized to a value between 0 and 1, multiplied
by a weight that depends on the kind of business, and summed into a final score.
This page explains it twice: once in plain English for the intuition, and once in
the formulas for the implementation.

<Note>
**Spec-contract discipline.** The default behavior follows the written spec
literally, even where a "smarter" alternative exists. Alternatives (a smoothed
rating, a distance decay curve) ship as config switches that default off. We do
not silently substitute a cleverer formula for the one we were asked to build.
See [ADR-0004](https://github.com/danielreales00/lemon-search/blob/main/docs/adr/0004-spec-contract-discipline.md)
and the [distance options](#the-distance-option-literal-vs-decay) below.
</Note>

## The seven signals

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="In plain English">
    Each result earns points on seven things. How much each one matters depends
    on the type of business (see archetypes below).

    1. **Distance.** Closer is better, up to a 30-mile cap. Beyond that it counts
       for nothing.
    2. **Rating.** Higher-rated businesses score higher. New businesses get a
       slight rating demote so a brand-new spot does not leapfrog a proven one on
       a thin score.
    3. **Popularity.** More reviews means more confidence, but on a gentle curve.
       A place with 800 reviews should not bury one with 50; it just gets a
       modest edge.
    4. **Friends.** If your friends reacted positively, the business gets a boost.
       More friends, bigger boost.
    5. **Claimed.** A business that has claimed its profile gets a meaningful
       boost; an unclaimed one gets none.
    6. **Photos.** Three or more photos is full credit. Fewer than three is a
       significant demotion, because a sparse listing looks worse and is usually
       less trustworthy.
    7. **Open now.** Open beats opens-later beats closed, computed from the
       business's posted hours against the current time. For some business types,
       being closed removes it from the results entirely.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="The math">
    The final score is a weighted linear sum, where the weights come from the
    business's archetype:

    ```text
    score(c) = sum over signals of  weight_i[archetype(c)] * signal_i(c)
    ```

    Each `signal_i(c)` is in the range 0 to 1. The seven signal formulas (default
    "literal" mode):

    ```text
    distance     = max(1 - distance_km / 48.28, 0)         # 48.28 km is 30 miles
    rating       = (lemon_score / 10) * (0.85 if is_new else 1.0)
    popularity   = log(1 + review_count) / log(1 + 10000)
    friends      = min(friend_count / 5, 1.0)
    claimed      = 1.0 if is_claimed else 0.0
    photos       = 1.0 if photo_count >= 3 else 0.25
    open_status  = 1.0 if open_now,  0.3 if opens-later,  0.0 if closed,  0.7 if hours unknown
    ```

    Notes that matter:

    - The score is **not** normalized; its theoretical maximum is the sum of the
      archetype's weights. Tie-breaking and de-pin distances work in absolute
      score space.
    - Distance is computed by Postgres at retrieval time with
      `earth_distance(loc, user_loc)`. A business with no coordinates gets
      distance infinity, so its distance signal is forced to 0.
    - The 0.85 new-business factor lives in
      `config.new_business.rating_demote_factor`. The popularity denominator is a
      fixed `GLOBAL_MAX_REVIEWS` (10000) so behavior is stable across data
      updates.
    - Hours-unknown rows get 0.7 (soft-open) and are **never** hard-filtered: a
      conservative call so we do not hide the 19% of businesses with missing
      hours.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## The six archetypes

The same signal matters differently for different businesses. Distance is
everything for a tow truck and nearly irrelevant for a wedding photographer you
would happily drive across town for. So every category maps to one of six
archetypes, and each archetype has its own set of weights.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="In plain English">
    | Archetype | Examples | What it leans on |
    |---|---|---|
    | Low-stakes, fast and nearby | restaurants, cafés, bars, fast food | distance, rating, open-now is a hard filter |
    | Medium-stakes, occasion | gyms, salons, barbers, spas, tattoo | rating, popularity, friends; distance moderate |
    | High-stakes, one-time | weddings, contractors, photographers, movers | rating, claimed, friends, photos; distance barely matters |
    | Experiential | boats, hotels, theaters, beach clubs | photo quality, rating; open-now nearly irrelevant |
    | Recurring service | cleaners, dog walkers, meal prep, coworking | rating, claimed, friends; distance as a service-area filter |
    | Utility, distance-dominant | towing, tires, glass repair, convenience | distance extremely high, open-now hard filter |

    Two examples make the intuition concrete. For a **café**, you want the closest
    good one that is open right now, so distance is heavy and being closed removes
    it. For a **wedding photographer**, you will travel and you book weeks ahead,
    so distance and open-now barely count while rating, reviews, and a claimed,
    photo-rich profile dominate.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="The math">
    Weights live in `config/ranking.yaml` and are the only place archetype
    behavior is encoded. The defaults:

    | Signal | low_stakes | medium_stakes | high_stakes | experiential | recurring | utility |
    |---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
    | distance | 0.25 | 0.10 | 0.04 | 0.08 | 0.08 | 0.45 |
    | rating | 0.18 | 0.20 | 0.20 | 0.22 | 0.20 | 0.10 |
    | popularity | 0.15 | 0.18 | 0.18 | 0.14 | 0.18 | 0.10 |
    | friends | 0.12 | 0.15 | 0.16 | 0.16 | 0.16 | 0.02 |
    | claimed | 0.01 | 0.03 | 0.12 | 0.07 | 0.10 | 0.08 |
    | photos | 0.10 | 0.12 | 0.15 | 0.25 | 0.10 | 0.05 |
    | open_status | 0.15 | 0.10 | 0.02 | 0.05 | 0.06 | 0.20 |
    | **open behavior** | hard_filter | soft | ignore | ignore | soft | hard_filter |

    The "big boost for claimed" the spec asks for lives entirely in the weight,
    and it is ordered as the spec implies: `claimed` is 0.12 for high-stakes and
    0.10 for recurring service, but only a tiebreaker (0.01) for low-stakes. The
    open-status behavior column controls whether a closed business is dropped
    before scoring (`hard_filter`), participates normally (`soft`), or is ignored
    (`ignore`).

    These claimed weights are the result of a measured sweep, not a first guess.
    The synthesized claimed flag is independent of every other signal, so a large
    claimed weight pulls unrelated claimed businesses to the top of every result
    set. A ranking-quality harness caught this (a 14-review claimed coffee cart
    outranking Panther Coffee with 2,550 reviews), and the sweep trimmed claimed
    across the board with the freed weight moved into popularity and rating.
    Result: aggregate `claimed_pct` over the 25-query set fell from 66% to 38%
    while category precision and mean rating both ticked up. The full
    before/after table and the story are in
    [Decisions and judgment](/decisions#claimed-and-friends-synthesize-both-then-sweep-the-weight).
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## The distance option: literal vs decay

Distance is the clearest place where a "smarter" formula was tempting, so it is
the model case for spec-contract discipline.

<Tabs>
  <Tab title="In plain English">
    The spec says "inverse distance, capped at 30 miles", which we read as a
    straight line: full credit up close, zero credit at 30 miles, linear in
    between. The per-archetype emphasis (utility cares about distance far more
    than experiential) lives in the **weight**, not the curve.

    An arguably better model is a decay curve that falls off sharply for a tow
    truck and gently for a destination spa. That is implemented and available, but
    it is **off by default**, because the literal reading is what the spec asked
    for. We ship the alternative behind a switch and quote a measured comparison
    rather than quietly swapping it in.
  </Tab>
  <Tab title="The math">
    ```text
    # literal (default)
    distance = max(1 - distance_km / 48.28, 0)

    # decay (config switch: signal_formulas.distance = decay)
    distance = exp(-distance_km / decay_km[archetype])
    ```

    Per-archetype decay constants (kilometers): utility 3, low-stakes 8,
    medium-stakes 16, recurring 16, experiential 48, high-stakes 80.

    A parallel switch exists for rating: `literal` (`lemon_score / 10`) versus
    `bayesian` (an IMDb-style smoothing of `google_rating`, useful because
    `lemon_score` is skewed with a mean near 9 and barely discriminates). Both
    switches default to literal; the bench runner exercises both modes and the
    writeup quotes the side-by-side.
  </Tab>
</Tabs>

## The pipeline around the score

The linear sum is the middle of an eight-step pipeline. The other steps handle
the spec's harder requirements:

<Steps>
  <Step title="Hard-filter pre-pass">
    For archetypes that hard-filter on hours (low-stakes, utility), drop any
    candidate explicitly closed now. This runs in Go, not SQL, because one query
    returns mixed archetypes and a single WHERE clause cannot express
    archetype-specific filtering.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Compute signals and sum">
    The seven signals and the weighted linear sum, as above.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Exact-name pin">
    If the query is an exact (possibly misspelled) business name, that business is
    pinned to position one with an effectively infinite score, honoring the spec's
    "returns that business first, regardless of other ranking signals". This is
    guarded carefully so a category word like "coffee" does not pin a business
    literally named "Coffee To Go". See
    [Smart intent and semantic search](/intent-and-semantic#the-exact-name-pin-and-its-guards).
  </Step>
  <Step title="Tie-break and de-pin">
    Equal scores break deterministically (claimed first, then closer, then more
    reviews, then stable by id). A final de-pin pass keeps brand-new businesses
    out of the very top two unless they clearly dominate, honoring "do not surface
    a new business at the very top".
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Worked example

Query "sushi near brickell", a sushi restaurant 1.5 km away, lemon score 9.2, 420
reviews, 2 friends, claimed, 8 photos, open now. Archetype is low-stakes:

```text
distance:    0.25 * 0.969 = 0.2422
rating:      0.18 * 0.920 = 0.1656
popularity:  0.15 * 0.656 = 0.0984
friends:     0.12 * 0.400 = 0.0480
claimed:     0.01 * 1.000 = 0.0100
photos:      0.10 * 1.000 = 0.1000
open_status: 0.15 * 1.000 = 0.1500
                            ------
score                      = 0.8142
```

A similar sushi place 4 km away with 50 reviews and no friends scores about
0.7188. The first wins by roughly 0.10: distance dominates within the
neighborhood-tight low-stakes weights, but popularity and friends are the
differentiators between two close restaurants. Note how small the claimed term
is for low-stakes (0.01): post-sweep, claimed is a tiebreaker here, not a sort
key.

For the full formula reference (every edge case, the bench numbers), see
[docs/ranking/semantics.md](https://github.com/danielreales00/lemon-search/blob/main/docs/ranking/semantics.md)
in the repo.
